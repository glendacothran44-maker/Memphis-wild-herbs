Wild Herbs Growing Around Memphis

These plants spread naturally through wind, foot traffic, and wildlife across the Memphis area. Browse each herb to discover its traditional uses, health benefits, and edible features.

🌿
Broadleaf Plantain
Plantago major
Also known as: Common Plantain, Dooryard Weed
Edible Medicinal Topical
About this plant

One of the most widespread plants in Memphis — found in lawns, roadsides, and disturbed soil. Its ribbed oval leaves are a giveaway. Seeds spread by wind and foot traffic.

Traditional health benefits
  • Soothes insect bites, stings, and minor skin irritations when leaf is crushed and applied
  • Traditionally used to support respiratory health and soothe coughs
  • Anti-inflammatory properties used in folk medicine for wound care
  • Rich in vitamins A, C, and K
🍽️ Edible: Young leaves can be eaten raw in salads (mild, slightly bitter) or cooked like spinach. Seeds can be ground into flour. Leaves get tougher and more bitter with age.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Avoid if allergic to plantain pollen.
Best found:Spring – Fall
🌱
Purslane
Portulaca oleracea
Also known as: Pigweed, Little Hogweed, Verdolaga
Edible Medicinal
About this plant

A low-growing succulent with thick, fleshy reddish stems and small oval leaves. Thrives in Memphis heat — often spotted in garden beds, driveways, and vacant lots in summer.

Traditional health benefits
  • One of the richest plant sources of omega-3 fatty acids (alpha-linolenic acid)
  • High in antioxidants including vitamins C, E, and beta-carotene
  • Traditionally used to support heart health and reduce inflammation
  • Contains melatonin — may support healthy sleep patterns
  • Good source of magnesium, potassium, and calcium
🍽️ Edible: Entire plant above ground is edible. Leaves and stems have a mild, slightly lemony, slightly mucilaginous flavor. Great raw in salads, sautéed, or added to soups. A staple green in Mediterranean cooking.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. High in oxalates — use in moderation if prone to kidney stones.
Best found:Summer – Early Fall
🌾
Goldenrod
Solidago spp.
Also known as: Solidago, Woundwort, Aaron's Rod
Medicinal Good for Tea Topical
About this plant

Tall, bright yellow plumes that light up Memphis roadsides and fields in late summer and fall. A native Tennessee wildflower that spreads abundantly by wind-carried seeds. Often mistakenly blamed for hay fever (that's ragweed!).

Traditional health benefits
  • Traditionally used to support urinary tract and kidney health
  • Strong anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties
  • Used in European herbal medicine for respiratory support
  • May help relieve allergy symptoms (ironically — despite the confusion with ragweed)
  • Topically used in traditional medicine for wound healing and skin conditions
🍵 Tea: Dried flowers and leaves make a pleasant, mildly sweet herbal tea. Pour boiling water over 1–2 tsp dried herb, steep 10–15 minutes. Flowers are also edible as a garnish.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Avoid if you have ragweed allergies or kidney disease. Not recommended during pregnancy without medical guidance.
Best found:Late Summer – Fall
🌿
Cleavers
Galium aparine
Also known as: Sticky Weed, Goosegrass, Catchweed, Velcro Plant
Edible Medicinal Good for Tea Tonic
About this plant

The plant that sticks to your clothes! Cleavers scramble through hedgerows and roadsides across Memphis in spring. Its sticky hooks spread seeds by hitching rides on animals and people.

Traditional health benefits
  • Historically one of the most used lymphatic system tonics in Western herbalism
  • Traditionally used to support healthy skin, including eczema and psoriasis
  • Used as a gentle diuretic to support kidney and urinary health
  • Rich in tannins and flavonoids with antioxidant properties
  • Traditional spring tonic — used for detoxification and cleansing after winter
🍽️ Edible: Young spring shoots can be eaten raw or lightly cooked (steaming reduces stickiness). Seeds can be dried and roasted as a caffeine-free coffee substitute. Best harvested in early spring before flowering.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. May interact with blood thinners or diabetes medications. Avoid large quantities during pregnancy.
Best found:Early Spring
🌼
Dandelion
Taraxacum officinale
Also known as: Lion's Tooth, Piss-en-lit, Blowball
Edible Medicinal Good for Tea Tonic
About this plant

Memphis lawns and fields are full of dandelions. Every part of this plant is useful. Seeds travel up to 5 miles on the wind — one of the most effective wind-dispersed plants in the world.

Traditional health benefits
  • Excellent source of vitamins A, C, K, and B vitamins — more nutritious than many cultivated greens
  • Traditionally used to support liver and gallbladder health
  • Natural diuretic that may support kidney function while replenishing potassium
  • Prebiotic fiber (inulin) supports healthy gut flora
  • Anti-inflammatory properties studied for blood sugar and cholesterol support
🍽️ Edible: Entire plant is edible. Young leaves are great raw in salads (bitter, similar to arugula) or sautéed with garlic. Flowers can be eaten raw, battered and fried, or made into dandelion wine. Roots can be roasted as a coffee alternative or boiled as a vegetable.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. May interact with diuretics, lithium, and certain antibiotics. Avoid if allergic to ragweed, chrysanthemums, or daisies.
Best found:Year-round
🌸
Red Clover
Trifolium pratense
Also known as: Meadow Clover, Cow Clover, Beebread
Edible Medicinal Good for Tea Tonic
About this plant

Pink-purple globe-shaped flowers dot Memphis meadows, roadsides, and lawns from spring through fall. Seeds spread by wind and pollinators. A beloved plant for bees and herbalists alike.

Traditional health benefits
  • Contains isoflavones (plant estrogens) — traditionally used for menopausal symptom support
  • Traditionally used to support respiratory health and ease coughs
  • Rich in calcium, magnesium, potassium, and vitamins C and B
  • Used as a traditional blood purifier and lymphatic tonic
  • Anti-inflammatory and antioxidant compounds studied for cardiovascular support
🍽️ Edible: Fresh flowers are sweet and edible — great in salads or as a garnish. Dried flowers make a pleasant, mildly sweet floral tea. Young leaves can be eaten raw or cooked. Seeds can be sprouted.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Contains plant estrogens — avoid if you have hormone-sensitive conditions or are on blood thinners or hormone therapy.
Best found:Spring – Fall
🌿
Chickweed
Stellaria media
Also known as: Starwort, Winterweed, Satin Flower
Edible Medicinal Topical
About this plant

A delicate, low-growing plant with tiny star-shaped white flowers. Thrives in cool weather — look for it in Memphis lawns and shady garden beds in late winter through spring.

Traditional health benefits
  • Traditionally used topically to soothe itchy skin, rashes, eczema, and insect bites
  • Mild anti-inflammatory properties support skin health
  • Rich in vitamins C, A, and B vitamins, plus iron and calcium
  • Traditionally used as a gentle remedy for respiratory irritation
  • Used in folk medicine to support healthy weight management
🍽️ Edible: One of the tastiest wild greens — mild, fresh flavor similar to spinach. Eat raw in salads, on sandwiches, or lightly steamed. Best harvested young in late winter and early spring before it flowers.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Eat in moderate amounts only. Avoid if taking blood thinners or diuretics.
Best found:Late Winter – Spring
🍀
Wood Sorrel
Oxalis stricta
Also known as: Yellow Oxalis, Sour Weed, Lemon Clover
Edible Medicinal
About this plant

Heart-shaped trifoliate leaves that look like clover but taste like lemon! Small yellow flowers and seed pods that explode when ripe to spread seeds. Very common in Memphis yards and shady spots.

Traditional health benefits
  • High in vitamin C — historically used to prevent and treat scurvy
  • Traditionally used to stimulate appetite and aid digestion
  • Cooling and refreshing — used traditionally to reduce fevers
  • Mild antibacterial properties noted in traditional medicine
🍽️ Edible: Leaves, flowers, and seed pods all have a bright, lemony-sour flavor from oxalic acid. Great as a trail snack, added to salads, or used as a garnish. The sour flavor also works well in drinks and sauces.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. High in oxalic acid — eat in small amounts only. Not recommended for those with kidney stones, gout, or rheumatoid arthritis.
Best found:Spring – Fall
