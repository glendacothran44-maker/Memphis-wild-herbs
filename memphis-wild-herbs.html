Broadleaf Plantain
Plantago major
Also known as: Common Plantain, Dooryard Weed
EdibleMedicinalTopical
About this plant

One of the most widespread plants in Memphis — found in lawns, roadsides, and disturbed soil. Its ribbed oval leaves are a giveaway. Seeds spread by wind and foot traffic.

Traditional health benefits
  • Soothes insect bites, stings, and minor skin irritations when leaf is crushed and applied
  • Traditionally used to support respiratory health and soothe coughs
  • Anti-inflammatory properties used in folk medicine for wound care
  • Rich in vitamins A, C, and K
🍽️ Edible: Young leaves can be eaten raw in salads or cooked like spinach. Seeds can be ground into flour.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Avoid if allergic to plantain pollen.
Best found:Spring – Fall

Purslane
Portulaca oleracea
Also known as: Pigweed, Little Hogweed, Verdolaga
EdibleMedicinal
About this plant

A low-growing succulent with thick, fleshy reddish stems and small oval leaves. Thrives in Memphis heat — often spotted in garden beds, driveways, and vacant lots in summer.

Traditional health benefits
  • One of the richest plant sources of omega-3 fatty acids
  • High in antioxidants including vitamins C, E, and beta-carotene
  • Traditionally used to support heart health and reduce inflammation
  • Contains melatonin — may support healthy sleep patterns
🍽️ Edible: Entire plant above ground is edible. Mild, slightly lemony flavor. Great raw in salads, sautéed, or added to soups.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. High in oxalates — use in moderation if prone to kidney stones.
Best found:Summer – Early Fall
© Wikimedia Commons CC BY-SA
Goldenrod
Solidago spp.
Also known as: Solidago, Woundwort, Aaron's Rod
MedicinalGood for TeaTopical
About this plant

Tall, bright yellow plumes that light up Memphis roadsides and fields in late summer and fall. A native Tennessee wildflower — often mistakenly blamed for hay fever (that's ragweed!).

Traditional health benefits
  • Traditionally used to support urinary tract and kidney health
  • Strong anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties
  • Used in European herbal medicine for respiratory support
  • Topically used in traditional medicine for wound healing
🍵 Tea: Dried flowers and leaves make a pleasant, mildly sweet herbal tea. Steep 10–15 minutes.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Avoid if you have ragweed allergies or kidney disease.
Best found:Late Summer – Fall
© Wikimedia Commons CC BY-SA
Cleavers
Galium aparine
Also known as: Sticky Weed, Goosegrass, Catchweed, Velcro Plant
EdibleMedicinalGood for TeaTonic
About this plant

The plant that sticks to your clothes! Cleavers scramble through hedgerows and roadsides across Memphis in spring. Its sticky hooks spread seeds by hitching rides on animals and people.

Traditional health benefits
  • Historically one of the most used lymphatic system tonics in Western herbalism
  • Traditionally used to support healthy skin including eczema and psoriasis
  • Used as a gentle diuretic to support kidney and urinary health
  • Traditional spring tonic used for detoxification after winter
🍽️ Edible: Young spring shoots can be eaten raw or lightly cooked. Seeds can be dried and roasted as a caffeine-free coffee substitute.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. May interact with blood thinners. Avoid large quantities during pregnancy.
Best found:Early Spring

Dandelion
Taraxacum officinale
Also known as: Lion's Tooth, Blowball, Piss-en-lit
EdibleMedicinalGood for TeaTonic
About this plant

Memphis lawns and fields are full of dandelions. Every part of this plant is useful. Seeds travel up to 5 miles on the wind — one of the most effective wind-dispersed plants in the world.

Traditional health benefits
  • Excellent source of vitamins A, C, K, and B vitamins
  • Traditionally used to support liver and gallbladder health
  • Natural diuretic that may support kidney function
  • Prebiotic fiber supports healthy gut flora
🍽️ Edible: Entire plant is edible. Young leaves great raw in salads. Flowers can be eaten raw or made into dandelion wine. Roots can be roasted as a coffee alternative.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. May interact with diuretics and certain antibiotics.
Best found:Year-round

Red Clover
Trifolium pratense
Also known as: Meadow Clover, Cow Clover, Beebread
EdibleMedicinalGood for TeaTonic
About this plant

Pink-purple globe-shaped flowers dot Memphis meadows, roadsides, and lawns from spring through fall. A beloved plant for bees and herbalists alike.

Traditional health benefits
  • Contains isoflavones — traditionally used for menopausal symptom support
  • Traditionally used to support respiratory health and ease coughs
  • Rich in calcium, magnesium, potassium, and vitamins C and B
  • Used as a traditional blood purifier and lymphatic tonic
🍽️ Edible: Fresh flowers are sweet and edible in salads. Dried flowers make a pleasant floral tea. Young leaves can be eaten raw or cooked.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Contains plant estrogens — avoid if you have hormone-sensitive conditions.
Best found:Spring – Fall
© Wikimedia Commons CC BY-SA
Chickweed
Stellaria media
Also known as: Starwort, Winterweed, Satin Flower
EdibleMedicinalTopical
About this plant

A delicate, low-growing plant with tiny star-shaped white flowers. Thrives in cool weather — look for it in Memphis lawns and shady garden beds in late winter through spring.

Traditional health benefits
  • Traditionally used topically to soothe itchy skin, rashes, and insect bites
  • Rich in vitamins C, A, and B vitamins, plus iron and calcium
  • Traditionally used as a gentle remedy for respiratory irritation
🍽️ Edible: One of the tastiest wild greens — mild, fresh flavor similar to spinach. Eat raw in salads or lightly steamed. Best harvested young in late winter.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. Eat in moderate amounts only.
Best found:Late Winter – Spring
© Wikimedia Commons CC BY-SA
Wood Sorrel
Oxalis stricta
Also known as: Yellow Oxalis, Sour Weed, Lemon Clover
EdibleMedicinal
About this plant

Heart-shaped trifoliate leaves that look like clover but taste like lemon! Small yellow flowers and seed pods that explode when ripe. Very common in Memphis yards and shady spots.

Traditional health benefits
  • High in vitamin C — historically used to prevent and treat scurvy
  • Traditionally used to stimulate appetite and aid digestion
  • Cooling and refreshing — used traditionally to reduce fevers
🍽️ Edible: Leaves, flowers, and seed pods all have a bright, lemony-sour flavor. Great as a trail snack or added to salads.
⚠️ Consult your doctor before use. High in oxalic acid — eat in small amounts only. Not recommended for those with kidney stones or gout.
Best found:Spring – Fall
